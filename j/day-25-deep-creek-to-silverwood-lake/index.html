Day 25: Deep Creek to Silverwood Lake

May 17, 2015 Rick AKA Apollo LinkedIn Home Leave a comment

Our 25th day on the PCT. We left Deep Creek, passed the Mojave River dam and forks, and camped at Silverwood Lake State Recreation Area. We were amazed, or just a little creeped out, by the poor condition of Silverwood Lake.

—
May 13th, 2015
Start location: Near mile 310, a small beach just after the rainbow bridge with a steep access trail.
End location: About mile 330, a seemingly abandoned equestrian campground just after the Silverwood Lake Cleghorn Picnic Area.
Miles hiked: Around 20

As always, we weren’t moving till 8 or 9am. We had a very steep climb out of the campsite at which we camped: it was about 100feet lower than the trail.

The first few miles featured a gradual descent towards the Mojave River dam along Deep Creek. This area (from Deep Creek Hot Springs to the Mojave River Dam) is frequented by locals and has a lot of graffiti and litter. The area is popular with fishermen and day-use swimmers alike; there were many access trails down to the water’s level (the PCT stayed roughly 100feet above water level along the side of the canyon).

Graffiti north of mile 310 along Deep Creek on the PCT

Graffiti on the PCT north of mile 310, along Deep Creek

When we reached the Mojave Dam, we met a couple our age from Utah that we continued to leapfrog throughout the day.

The Mojave Dam is interesting. Deep Creek flows into it from the southeast; the West Fork of the Mojave river flows into it from the west. The water flows north into the dam immediately at the point where Deep Creek meets the West Fork.

Erica in front of Mojave Dam

Deep Creek (flowing west) meets the west fork of the Mojave River (flowing east). They combine and flow north, immediately into the Mojave Dam.

After filling up on water from the West Fork (no, we wouldn’t drink from the water in Deep Creek- too much litter, vandalism, and improper poo handling), we ascended a ridgeline on which we stayed for most of the afternoon. It was a bit hilly, but had great views towards Hesperia.

View towards Hesperia from north of SR-173

Later in the afternoon, we descended that ridgeline. Once SR-138 was immediately adjacent to the trail, we were surprised (literally) to look left and see a large dam. As it turns out, that was Cedar Springs Dam. It creates Silverwood lake, a 70,000 acre feet State Water Project (SWP) reservoir.

Cedar Springs dam, which creates Silverwood Lake. View is from on the PCT.

Rick trying to push a large concrete cylinder. No luck.

We walked along SR-138 for a bit. This was a very ugly section of trail. Its only redeeming qualities were the Cedar Springs Dam’s spillway, and the Mojave Siphon power plant (a SWP plant).

We turned away from SR-138 and immediately started switchbacks up to the lake’s water level. Once at that level, we were surprised by the water level in the lake; it being a drought year, we had both expected to see a low water level, but it appeared normal. We walked along the perimeter of the lake for awhile, eventually ending up alongside SR-138 again. Rick waded into the lake for a bit during a break and water-fill-up session.

Erica in front of Silverwood Lake

We continued on a short distance to the Silverwood Lake Recreation Area’s Cleghorn Picnic Area. We were amazed by the state the Cleghorn Picnic Area was in. Clearly overbuilt, facilities, roadways, and amenities appeared abandoned. A bike path ran through the area; its pavement was split with years of plants growing through it. At least the water was on at a few of the spigots, despite toilets not flushing.

We ate dinner there at Cleghorn with another of other hikers we’d been seeing throughout the day. A large rabbit, the largest we’d yet seen on the trail, joined us for dinner. It was weird.

Abandoned-looking bike path near Cleghorn picnic area

Abandoned-looking bike path near Cleghorn picnic area

Very big rabbit near Silverwood Lake

We continued on past Cleghorn for no more than another mile. We stopped at an equestrian campground just after the PCT undercrossing with SR-138, along Cleghorn Road. Like the rest of the park’s amenities at Cleghorn Picnic area, this equestrian facility appeared abandoned. We belive it was part of “Rio Barranca Valle” campground.

An equestrian camping area we believed to be part of “Rio Barrance Valle” campground

We struggled to pitch the tent that night. It wouldn’t square properly in the high winds we were facing. It blew over once in the night, but otherwise we faired fine (we love the tent– the pitch was terrible).
